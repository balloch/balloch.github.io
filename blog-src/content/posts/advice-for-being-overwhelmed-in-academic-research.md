![medium_image](https://miro.medium.com/v2/resize:fit:1000/format:webp/1*YvYxp5Yg3aZMiq7M3T8uUw.jpeg)

I was recently told by a younger colleague that they were feeling very overwhelmed, and asked by them what I do to cope. Here is my unabridged response. May it help as many of you as possible.

---

The bad news is: for most PhDs, the amount of work that generates the feeling you are having now is extremely common. The good news is, there are very productive ways to cope with it.
I myself do indeed feel overwhelmed pretty much all the time. Most of my friends do too. The first good (but kinda bad?) thing that helps is: when it becomes normal you get a little numb to it. Nothing hurts the psyche as much as those first few weeks of feeling overwhelmed, before it normalizes.

The second thing that really helps is perspective. This is harder for some people (like me!) than it is for others. Its important to realize that, while what we do as researchers is important, its not that important, to you as a person or to the world at large. You can stop at any time, and neither the world nor your career will implode. Internalizing this message is WAY harder than actually understanding it, and even after many years, I have entire weeks where I can't talk myself down and everything feels critical and I always feel behind and like I'm letting everyone down, including myself. In those moments, you need to lean on your support structures. Talk to friends. Make plans to see people for dinner, or a vacation later. Talk to family and hear about their life. All of these things will remind you that, compared to all that stuff, all this work and overwork is relatively unimportant, which lowers the stakes of not getting it done.

The third thing that helps - more for some people than others - is structure. This isn't for everyone, and even for people who benefit from it, different structures work for different people. Some people like Professor Devi Parikh advocate for blocking out 100% of their calendar, including with "fun time" and other non-work stuff. This is something close to a nightmare for me. On the other hand, I do benefit from knowing, "What comes next?" and, "When is it needed by?" So usually with a colleague (or just me by myself) I'll try to break down what needs to happen in the short term to meet a goal in the long term, and do my best to give myself bite-sized tasks that I don't feel as overwhelmed by. By having a plan, not only is it easier to "say no" to additional obligations, but I also can avoid being overwhelmed by the multitude of distant, future goals; I can just think about "whats next?"

The fourth thing that helps - related to the last - is having a process-oriented mindset or mantra. For me, my current mantra (it changes a lot lol) is: "Wake up and keep swimming!" This was born out of a trend where I would often tell my therapist that I would then spend each and every day just treading water to keep from drowning. And she told me, "You need to stop that. If you keep treading you will drown. You need to put your energy into getting yourself out of this scenario. You need to swim, not tread." This is especially important in research because academia is a "sick system:" the rewards will be few and far between, you will feel like you cannot leave, you will be fully beholden to the people above you, and you will be too tired and busy to think, often feeling like you are constantly in crisis or damage control mode. We the students can do nothing about this; academia is fully broken for students. So the best we can do is stay committed to the process and detached from the results. For that means: don't worry if your results aren't coming in fast enough, or if you miss deadlines, or if you procrastinated, or if your first draft needs to be fully thrown out and written again. Keep researching, keep writing, and keep shipping work. This is, of course, way easier said than done. Sometimes advisors will help but sometimes they will hinder. Sometimes departments will tell you that if you don't get more papers out your funding will be pulled. And sometimes its just impossible to get out of your own head. Its really hard to just "not worry" about the results like rejections. But with time, practice, and reminders like mantras, anyone can "keep swimming."

The fifth thing, and one of the hardest ones to implement but ultimately one of the most important, is to know and understand you limits, and focus on living your life in a low-stakes way outside of that limit. This is not only really hard because of the expectations put on us by family, society, etc., but also because that limit is REALLY ambiguous and fickle. It changes based on mood, based on deadlines, based on time of year, based on how old you are, based on how much "work momentum" you have. But we owe it to ourselves, to try to estimate our limit, and when we are beyond it, spend the rest of that time doing things where the consequence have no big effect on our anxiety. Often times, the biggest barrier here for ambitious people is that we have taught ourselves to basically never say "no" to any opportunity that could benefit us. This is doubly hard for people like me (and you) that have many non-overlapping interests. However, as you continue to succeed, more and more potentially beneficial opportunities will be offered, and it becomes important to start weighing these opportunities and declining the ones that benefit you less. A tough example from my own life: was it a good opportunity for me last summer to intern at [big tech company]? Sure. If I hadn't done it, could I have potentially put enough time in to have saved the [recently lost academic grant I was leading], making my PhD a lot easier for me? Maybe. The more you learn to say no to things, the less likely it becomes that you will be overwhelmed.

The last, and maybe most important thing is to forgive yourself. With the [big tech company] example, any time it comes up, to make sure it doesn't get added to the pile of things overwhelming me, I need to actively remind myself that I did the best I could in the moment. There were large potential benefits by taking that internship that were ultimately unrealized, and I didn't know how much trouble we would have with the [recently lost grant] work, nor could I have anticipated the degree to which the PhD committee would be up my ass. And this applies to being overwhelmed by committing to too many things. If that ever happens to me, I always have to tell myself, "I'm going to have to half-ass a lot of that stuff, I'm going to do the best I can with the sanity I have left, and even though its true it might not be enough for other people, it will be enough for me. I'll be fine as long as I get through it."

---

Life in general can be extremely overwhelming, and as much as it often feels and looks like academia is unique in this way, it isn't really. It just feels awful because it forces us to "jump into the deep end" of learning a lot of life lessons really quickly, and because it feels - and often is - avoidable. But if all the above advice can be summed up in one thought it would be: you are not alone, other people have and will continue to go through what you are going through, and sympathetic listener will reassure you that you are in fact enough to rise to this challenge and get through it.

Stay [mentally] healthy y'all.
